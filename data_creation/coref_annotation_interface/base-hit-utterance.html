You will be shown several sentences from a document. In the last sentence, we have highlighted a mention (a word or phrase) of an entity (a person, place, or thing). This entity mention may be a pronoun (such as "she" or "their") or something else. We need your help to find the immediately previous (most closely preceding) mention of the same entity, whether in the same sentence or in an earlier sentence. You may select a mention by clicking and dragging your mouse pointer over the words making up the mention.

When selecting the immediately previous mention of the entity, please follow these rules:

Select a mention of the same entity.

For example:

Joan usually comes to work early . She likes to get a head start on the day .

The highlighted entity mention in the last sentence is "She." There are two entity mentions in the preceding sentence, "Joan" and "work." We would ask you to select "Joan," because it refers to the same entity (the person named Joan) as "She" does:

Joan usually comes to work early . She likes to get a head start on the day .

Select the immediately previous mention of the entity.

For example:

Joan usually comes to work early . She wants a promotion , so she likes to get a head start on the day .

There are two mentions of the person named Joan before the highlighted mention, one in the first sentence and one in the second; we would ask you to select "She" (capitalized), the most closely preceding mention:

Joan usually comes to work early . She wants a promotion , so she likes to get a head start on the day .

Select all words in a mention.

For example:

The office manager usually comes to work early . She likes to get a head start on the day .

We would ask you to select the full mention "The office manager" by clicking and dragging over all three words, as opposed to selecting just "office manager" or "manager:"

The office manager usually comes to work early . She likes to get a head start on the day .

Include possessive words as entity mentions.

For example:

The office manager does n't find her job challenging enough . She comes to work early in hopes of getting promoted .

While the entity mentions in the first sentence may appear to be "The office manager" and "her job," we also consider the possessive pronoun "her" to be a mention of the person in question. So, we would ask you to select "her" as the immediately previous mention:

The office manager does n't find her job challenging enough . She comes to work early in hopes of getting promoted .

If there is no previous mention of the entity, select no previous mention (discussed next).

For example:

The office lights are on early in the morning . The office manager , Joan , likes to get a head start on the day .

We would ask you to click no previous mention (which will be discussed next) as there is no previous mention of Joan.

In these simplified examples, we have highlighted one entity mention at a time and asked for the immediately previous mention of that entity. In general, we will select one or more entity mentions in the last sentence (called query mentions) and ask for the immediately preceding mention for each one. We will display the list of query mentions below the text; the active query mention will be highlighted in the text and bolded in the list below. Dragging over text will select the immediately previous mention for the active query mention only; a different query mention may be activated by clicking on it in the list of query mentions, by using the provided "next query" and "previous query" buttons, or by pressing "Tab" or "Shift" + "Tab" on your keyboard.

Additionally, we will restrict the entity mention selections that can be submitted. When you click and drag to select an entity mention in the text, the allowed mentions (called candidate mentions) that overlap your selection will be listed in a panel to the right, above the "no previous mention" button. Click on a candidate mention to change your selection.

You could do so by clicking "(3) soft cheeses" in the right panel, pressing "3" on your keyboard, or clicking and dragging your mouse over "soft cheeses" in the text.

At any time, you may press "?" or "h" on your keyboard to see a list of keyboard shortcuts. To hide the keyboard shortcuts box, click outside the box or press "?" or "h" again.
